DGT HUM 101 Final Project

Extracurricular Access and Inequality in American Childhood

Our project explores whether extracurricular activities are truly “extra,” or whether they reveal deeper inequalities in children’s access to educational and developmental opportunities.

Read Our Data Critique

Project Overview

Using the U.S. Census Bureau’s Survey of Income and Program Participation (SIPP), our group examines how children’s participation in activities such as sports, clubs, lessons, and religious groups is shaped by family income, parental education, household structure, race/ethnicity, gender, and region.

Although extracurricular activities are often treated as optional parts of childhood, research suggests that they are connected to academic achievement, social development, emotional well-being, and long-term educational opportunity. Our project asks what patterns of participation can reveal about inequality beyond the classroom.

Our Dataset

We use the 2024 SIPP dataset, with a focus on children ages 6 to 17 and their reported participation in extracurricular activities.

Our Main Question

Are extracurricular activities truly “extra,” or do they reveal unequal access to educational and developmental opportunities?
