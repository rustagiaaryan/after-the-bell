DGT HUM 101 Final Project

Extracurricular Access and Inequality in American Childhood

Our project explores whether extracurricular activities are truly “extra,” or whether they reveal deeper inequalities in children’s access to educational and developmental opportunities.

Explore Our Findings

Project Overview

Using the U.S. Census Bureau’s Survey of Income and Program Participation (SIPP), our group examines how children’s participation in activities such as sports, clubs, lessons, and religious groups is shaped by family income, parental education, household structure, race/ethnicity, gender, and region.

Although extracurricular activities are often treated as optional parts of childhood, research suggests that they are connected to academic achievement, social development, emotional well-being, and long-term educational opportunity. Our project asks what patterns of participation can reveal about inequality beyond the classroom.

Our Dataset

We use the 2021 and 2024 SIPP dataset, with a focus on children ages 6 to 17 and their reported participation in extracurricular activities.

Our Main Question

How do household socioeconomic status, family structure, gender, and demographic factors (race/ethnicity, region) interact to shape children’s access to and participation in extracurricular activities over time?

Policy and Research Timeline

Period Category Event / Title Notes
1972 Civil rights law Title IX of the Education Amendments
  • Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972 is passed, prohibiting sex-based discrimination in federally funded education programs and activities.
  • Functionally, Title IX promotes equal access to school-based extracurricular activities, and is particularly relevant to sports.
1973-1990 Disability rights Section 504, IDEA, and the ADA
  • Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973, the Individuals with Disabilities Act (IDEA, 1975), and Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA, 1990) are passed, providing protections for disabled students and their participation in school-based extracurriculars.
  • Originally passed in 1975 and most recently reauthorized in 2004, the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) ensures that students with disabilities are provided with a free and appropriate public education.
1990 Federal funding Child Care and Development Block Grant Act of 1990
  • The Child Care and Development Block Grant Act of 1990 was established to subsidize child care expenses and frequently supports before and after-school care for youth.
  • These programs often support other enrichment and extracurricular activities.
1994 Federal funding 21st Century Community Learning Centers established
  • The Nita M. Lowey 21st Century Community Learning Centers funding grant was established in 1994 to fund State educational agencies identification of subprograms creating community learning centers.
  • Since its first first grant in 1998, CCLC has been used to fund enrichment activities and is one of the largest streams of national funding for extracurriculars.
2004 Research data SIPP Survey on youth extracurricular participation
  • The SIPP survey collected information about kids' extracurricular activities, family income, race, family structure, and how involved parents were in their children's lives (An & Western, 2019).
  • Researchers used this data to understand why some kids have more access to activities like sports, clubs, and lessons than others (An & Western, 2019).
2010s Research findings Equity gaps in extracurricular access documented
  • Research started showing that students from lower-income families joined extracurricular activities less often than wealthier students (An & Western, 2019).
  • Even though these students often benefited the most from participating, they usually had fewer opportunities.
  • Access to extracurriculars was affected by race and ethnicity, immigrant background, gender expectations, parent support, neighborhood and school resources (An & Western, 2019).
2020-2021 COVID-19 disruption Widespread shutdown of youth programs
  • Schools, sports teams, clubs, and community programs shut down across the country because of COVID-19 (Dunton et al., 2020).
  • Studies found that during the pandemic kids spent less time being active, screen time increased, many activities moved online or stopped completely (Dunton et al., 2020).
  • COVID showed how much family resources and social conditions affect whether kids can participate in extracurricular activities (An & Western, 2019).
2025+ Post-pandemic recovery Research focuses on social and academic recovery
  • After the pandemic, researchers focused on how students were recovering socially, emotionally, and academically (Xiao et al., 2024).
  • Studies looked at rebuilding extracurricular programs, mental health, motivation and confidence and social connections.
  • Research found that extracurricular activities helped students feel connected again after isolation (Xiao et al., 2024).
