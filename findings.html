Research Findings

Three Essential Questions About Extracurricular Access

These findings connect our main research questions to the patterns visible in the SIPP extracurricular participation data from 2020 and 2023.

Question 1

How do household socioeconomic status, family structure, gender, and demographic factors interact to shape children's access to and participation in extracurricular activities over time?

Parent education

Parent education shows one of the clearest access gaps. Children with at least one parent who had some college education or higher were more likely to participate in extracurriculars than children whose parents had a high school education or less. Participation increased in 2023 for both groups, but the education gap remained visible.

Question 1

Family type

Children in married-couple households had the highest extracurricular participation in both years, while children in single-parent households had lower rates. The female-no-spouse and male-no-spouse categories were both below married-couple households, with the female-no-spouse group generally lowest. Participation rose from 2020 to 2023 for all family types, but the relative ordering stayed the same. This suggests that household structure matters, not just family income or education. The differences may reflect variation in time, resources, supervision, or ability to pay for activities.

Question 1

Race and activity type

Participation also varies by race and by the type of activity being measured. The race visualization separates sports, clubs, lessons, after-school programs, and any extracurricular participation, which makes it easier to see that access is not distributed evenly across activity categories. The pattern points to both broad participation gaps and differences in the kinds of activities children enter.

Question 1

Poverty status

Poverty status is another important divider. Children not in poverty participated at higher rates than children in poverty in both years. The increase from 2020 to 2023 suggests partial recovery after the pandemic period, but the economic access gap did not disappear.

Question 1

Gender and activity type

Gender differences depend on the activity being measured. Looking at clubs, lessons, sports, before or after-school programs, and overall participation side by side shows that extracurricular access is shaped by both gender and the specific form of involvement.

Question 1

Region

Regional averages add a geographic layer to the access question. The regional map compares participation across the Northeast, Midwest, South, and West in both survey years, showing that extracurricular opportunity is also shaped by where children live.

Question 2

How sharply did extracurricular participation change during and after COVID (2020-2021)?

Age

Extracurricular participation rises sharply from the youngest ages and stays relatively high through early adolescence before tapering off in the later teen years. The 2023 line is generally above the 2020 line for most ages, which suggests stronger participation after the pandemic period. Participation seems highest around ages 11 to 13, then gradually declines by the oldest ages shown. That pattern likely reflects changing schedules, autonomy, and interest as children get older. The age gradient is not linear, but it is clearly patterned.

Question 3

How is extracurricular participation tied to academic involvement and achievement?

Repeated grade / Suspension

Students who repeated a grade or were suspended or expelled appear to have different extracurricular participation than students without those experiences. Participation is lower among students with these school disruption indicators, especially for the repeated-grade group. This suggests a connection between school behavior or academic difficulty and lower extracurricular engagement. The year difference is visible, but the school-disruption gap remains in both years. The plot implies that academic or disciplinary challenges may reduce access to structured activities.

Question 3

Homework engagement

Homework engagement gives another view of the relationship between academic involvement and extracurricular participation. Students who completed homework more consistently tended to have higher extracurricular participation, suggesting that structured academic and out-of-school engagement often move together.

Question 3

Cares about school

The pattern for caring about school reinforces the same academic engagement story. Students who were more connected to school generally participated in extracurriculars at higher rates, while lower school attachment was associated with lower extracurricular participation.
