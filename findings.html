After the Bell

Research Findings

Introduction

What happens after the school bell rings? For some children, sports teams, music lessons, tutoring, or an after-school enrichment program may be part of their afternoon. For others, these opportunities may be unavailable due to cost, family barriers, cultural background, or other factors. While these children may all have similar interests and aptitudes, the opportunity to participate in extracurricular activities is not equal. Indeed, extracurricular activities are oftentimes treated as optional to a child's education. However, these activities can shape much more than just a child's schedule. They can influence social connection, academic engagement, confidence, and access to future opportunities. Our project argues that extracurricular participation is not just some personal interest, but instead it highlights inequalities in policies, family resources, parental education, poverty, geography, and access to support systems. Examining who participates in extracurricular activities and the barriers to participation can help us understand how inequality manifests in childhood and inform efforts to create more equitable opportunities for all children.

To study these patterns, we use data from the U.S. Census Bureau's Survey of Income and Program Participation (SIPP), focusing especially on children ages 6 to 17 and their participation in sports, clubs, lessons, and before- or after-school programs. Because SIPP also includes household and demographic variables such as poverty status, parent education, family structure, gender, race, and Census region, it allows us to connect children's extracurricular participation to larger questions about educational opportunity in the United States. Our visualizations show that children with at least one parent who has some college or higher participate in extracurricular activities at higher rates than children whose parents have a high school education or less, and that children in poverty participate less in activities like sports, clubs, and lessons. These patterns suggest that extracurricular activities are not equally available to all children, and that inequality in education extends beyond the classroom.

Literature Review

Existing research generally agrees that extracurricular participation is connected to positive academic, social, and emotional outcomes for students. Scholars have found that students who participate in structured extracurricular activities often show stronger academic engagement, improved attendance, higher achievement, and lower dropout rates. Other studies explain that extracurriculars support student development by helping students build noncognitive and socioemotional skills, such as teamwork, persistence, responsibility, motivation, and sense of belonging (Covay and Carbonaro).

These skills matter because they connect students' experiences outside the classroom to their academic performance and long-term educational outcomes. For example, Covay and Carbonaro argue that structured extracurricular activities contribute to both noncognitive skills and academic achievement, while Morris shows that high school extracurricular participation can strengthen academic engagement and increase the likelihood of a four-year college attendance (Morris). Together this research suggests that extracurricular activities are not separate from education but are part of the broader set of opportunities that can shape students' success.

However, the literature also shows that these benefits are not equally available to all students. Several scholars find that socioeconomic status, race, gender, family structure, immigrant status, and parental involvement shape whether children are able to participate in extracurricular activities (Castillo; Heath et al.; Meier et al.). Students from higher-income families or two-parent households often have greater access to structured activities because they are more likely to have transportation, financial resources, and parent support (An and Western). In contrast, marginalized students may face barriers such as cost, time, and safety concerns (Castillo). Heat et al. also emphasize that disadvantaged youth are often less likely to participate even though they may benefit greatly from extracurricular involvement.

Other scholars, such as Dunton et al., highlight the effect of COVID-19. The study focuses on how the pandemic disrupted children's access to organized activities due to school, parks, and structured program closure. Results showed that the pandemic significantly decreased physical activity, mainly affecting older children and girls (Dunton et al.).

While existing research has examined factors such as socioeconomic status, race, gender, family structure, motivation, etc., many studies focus on these variables separately rather than analyzing how they interact. This leaves an important gap for our project. How do socioeconomic factors, family structure, gender, race, region, and time period, work together to shape children's extracurricular participation during and after the COVID pandemic? Our project builds on the existing literature by examining extracurricular participation as both an educational benefit and an unequal opportunity shaped by broader social and demographic factors.

Significance

While existing research has often studied factors such as poverty, socioeconomic status, gender, race or academic achievement separately, our project brings these variables together to show how they influence participation during and after the COVID pandemic. This project is important because it explores how demographic factors shape children's opportunities to participate in extracurricular activities and how those opportunities tie into academic outcomes over time. By examining extracurricular participation as both an educational benefit and an unequal opportunity shaped by broader social and demographic factors, our research builds on existing literature while highlighting disparities in access and participation.

We are working on this project because we want to find out how socioeconomic status, gender, race and other demographic factors influence children's participation in extracurricular activities. We are also interested in how these factors affected children's participation during and after COVID so that we can help others understand how unequal access to opportunities outside of the classroom may contribute to overall educational inequalities. In our project, we highlight how family resources and educational support shape children's experiences inside and outside of school by examining how extracurricular participation differs across different demographic factors and how it changes over time. This work is important as it connects extracurricular participation to larger questions about inequality and opportunity, which helps show that access to extracurricular activities can have lasting effects on educational achievement. By identifying which groups face barriers to extracurricular participation, this project can be used by educators and policymakers to promote programs and policies that improve access to extracurriculars and in turn long-term outcomes.

Research Question 1

How do household socioeconomic status, family structure, gender, and demographic factors (race/ethnicity, region) interact to shape children's access to and participation in extracurricular activities over time?

The first research question we explored seeks to understand what factors such as race, family type, and parental education level shape children's participation in extracurriculars. Based on our literature review, hypothesized that family type would influence extracurricular participation due to correlated variables like access to transportation and financial support. Indeed, our analysis supported this expectation, with participation rates varying across family types. We also explored whether parental education level was associated with extracurricular participation. Educational attainment is tied closely to socioeconomic factors, and given the benefits tied to extracurriculars, we expected that children of more highly educated parents would participate in extracurricular activities at a higher rate.

Research Question 2

How sharply did extracurricular participation change during and after COVID (2020-2021)?

Next, we wanted to assess whether rates of extracurricular participation were influenced during the COVID-19 pandemic. The longitudinal structure of the SIPP data lends itself particularly well to analyzing children's experiences during this period of disruption and recovery. While our subset of the data does not allow us to compare pre-pandemic to post-pandemic rates, it does allow us to compare participation during the pandemic in 2020 with participation during the recovery period in 2023. We found a substantial increase in extracurricular participation between the two years, suggesting that youth engagement in enrichment activities rebounded as normal operations resumed. By comparing these changes across additional factors like poverty and age, we can see whether pandemic recovery occurred equally across groups or whether new disparities arose during the transition period.

Research Question 3

How is extracurricular participation tied to academic involvement and achievement?

Finally, we wanted to investigate whether extracurricular participation was related to academic factors. While extracurricular activities may be seen as beneficial primarily for the personal growth and soft skills developed, prior research suggests that it is also linked to higher academic achievement. At the same time, participation has also been associated with fewer disciplinary issues. By examining SIPP variables related to academic performance and school disruption, we were able to confirm these patterns in our data.

Figure 8: Extracurricular Participation by School Disruption Type and Year

Interactive grouped bar chart comparing extracurricular participation for students with repeated grade or suspension and expulsion indicators against students without those school disruption indicators. Participation is lower for students with these experiences in both 2020 and 2023, though both groups rise in 2023.

This chart compares extracurricular participation among students who had repeated a grade and/or been suspended/expelled and those who did not repeat a grade and/or been suspended/expelled in 2020 and 2023. Students who repeated a grade and/or had been suspended/expelled had participation rates of 46.5% in 2020 and 55.5% in 2023, while students who had never repeated a grade or been suspended or expelled reported participation rates of 51.9% in 2020 and 61.3% in 2023. Participation increased for both groups between the two years, though rates remained lower for those students with these school disruption indicators (the repeated grade and suspended/expelled group). This pattern suggests that academic and disciplinary challenges may reduce students' opportunities or likelihood of participating in extracurricular activities.

Interactive grouped bar chart comparing extracurricular participation by homework engagement in 2020 and 2023. Categories range from doing homework all of the time to none of the time, plus not enrolled. This chart examines extracurricular participation by student-reported homework engagement levels in 2020 and 2023. The data were grouped into five categories: All of the time, most of the time, some of the time, none of the time, and not enrolled. Extracurricular participation increased across all homework engagement categories between the two years. Students reporting the highest homework engagement showed the highest extracurricular participation rates (53.5% in 2020 and 62.7% in 2023), while students reporting the lowest homework engagement (coded none of the time) had the lowest rates (40.4% in 2020 and 52.2% in 2023). This pattern suggests that extracurricular involvement is associated with academic engagement, indicating that students who are more invested in school-related obligations are also more likely to participate in extracurriculars.

Conclusion

Collectively, the data visualizations show that extracurricular participation increased from 2020 to 2023, suggesting recovery following the COVID-19 pandemic. However, participation across different socioeconomic, family, racial, and demographic groups remains highly uneven. Students who demonstrate stronger academic engagement through caring more about school and homework habits tend to participate more in extracurricular activities. These findings suggest that extracurricular activities are shaped by systemic inequalities as well as student educational experiences.
