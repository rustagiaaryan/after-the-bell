Research Sources

Annotated Bibliography

An, Weihua, and Bruce Western. “Social Capital in the Creation of Cultural Capital: Family Structure, Neighborhood Cohesion, and Extracurricular Participation.” Social Science Research, vol. 81, July 2019, pp. 192-208, https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ssresearch.2019.03.015.

Using longitudinal data from the 2004 Survey of Income and Program Participation, An and Western analyze how family structure and neighborhood cohesion influence children’s extracurricular participation. Drawing on James Coleman’s theory of social capital, they argue that extracurricular participation helps children develop cultural capital – social skills and cultural knowledge – that better equip them to succeed in their academics and careers. Analyzing extracurricular participation in sports, clubs, and religious activities as a dependent variable, An and Western find significantly higher rates of extracurricular participation among children in two-parent married households, cohesive neighborhoods, and higher-income families. While the article’s reliance on Coleman’s outdated framework may limit its applications to diverse contexts, this article is especially relevant for our research because it uses the same dataset, allowing us to compare our findings and to inform our selection of variables related to family contexts.

Castillo, Cynthia Alicia. Identifying Challenges to School-Based Extracurricular Participation for High School Students from the Perspective of Immigrant, Non-Immigrant Latinx, and Non-Immigrant Black Parents. 2024. University of California, Los Angeles, https://escholarship.org/uc/item/3qj1m3t7.

Castillo (2024) examines how immigrant, non-immigrant Latinx, and Black parents of LAUSD high school students view extracurricular activities and the barriers that limit their children’s participation. Using semi-structured interviews with 15 parents, Castillo (2024) explores parents’ awareness of benefits, sources of information, and challenges. She finds that all parents see extracurriculars as important for social ties and academic success. The study also shows that schools rarely explain the benefits of extracurriculars or actively recruit marginalized students, which leaves parents and students to navigate opportunities on their own. This work is important because it centers the voices of parents from groups that have the lowest extracurricular participation. For our project, Castillo’s findings directly support our focus on how socioeconomic status, immigration background, race or ethnicity, and family structure shape access to extracurriculars over time and help explain why low-income and immigrant youth participate less and therefore have fewer opportunities to gain academic and socioemotional benefits.

Covay, Elizabeth, and William Carbonaro. “After the Bell: Participation in Extracurricular Activities, Classroom Behavior, and Academic Achievement.” Sociology of Education, vol. 83, no. 1, January 2010, pp. 20-45, https://doi.org/10.1177/0038040709356565.

Covay and Carbonaro argue that participation in structured extracurricular activities contribute positively to students' noncognitive skills and academic achievement as well as explains some of the socioeconomic status advantages in education. The authors use data from the Early Childhood Longitudinal Study-Kindergarten Class of 1998-99, analyzing elementary school students’ classroom behaviors, math and English test scores using regression models. This resource is important because it shows that educational inequalities are also influenced by structured activities outside of school and not only family background or in the classroom. This research supports our thesis as it shows how extracurriculars are tied to academic engagement and success. It also directly examines some social and demographic factors (such as higher SES, family background) that predict extracurricular participation.

Dunton, Genevieve F., et al. “Early Effects of the COVID-19 Pandemic on Physical Activity and Sedentary Behavior in Children Living in the U.S.” BMC Public Health, vol. 20, no. 1, Dec. 2020, p. 1351, https://doi.org/10.1186/s12889-020-09429-3.

Dunton et al. (2020) looks at how COVID-19 restrictions affected physical activity (PA) and sedentary behavior (SB) among children ages 5-13 in the U.S. during the early period of the pandemic. In a survey of 211 parents across 35 states conducted between April and May 2020, the authors compare parent reported PA and SB before the pandemic (February) to the early-COVID period, looking at differences by gender, age, and other demographics. They argue that the closure of schools, parks, and youth sports leagues causes major decreases in PA and increases in SB, with older children (9 to 13) and girls experiencing the most changes, and that childrens’ PA switched from organized activities to unstructured free play at home and in neighborhood streets. The authors also mention how streaming services became a new form of extracurricular participation with nearly a third of children using these platforms for activity classes during the early COVID period. This source is important for our project because it finds an important disruption point in extracurricular participation, showing how external shocks can suddenly remove access to organized activities and shift participation patterns by age and gender. It supports our thesis by demonstrating that access to structured extracurriculars is effected not only by long term socioeconomic factors, but also by other structural conditions, and it brings up questions about whether the pandemic era shift to remote programming widened or narrowed disparities in access. A key limitation is the samples lack of diversity, as surveyees were mostly highly educated, non-Hispanic white mothers from higher income households, meaning the findings underrepresent how the pandemic affected low income and marginalized children, who Castillo (2024) and Heath et al. (2022) suggest already face the greatest barriers to extracurricular participation.

Darling, Nancy. “Participation in Extracurricular Activities and Adolescent Adjustment: Cross-Sectional and Longitudinal Findings.” Journal of Youth and Adolescence, vol. 34, no. 5, October 2005, pp. 493-505, https://doi.org/10.1007/s10964-005-7266-8.

The article argues that participation in school-based extracurricular activities is associated with more positive outcomes in adolescents. This included higher academic achievement and lower involvement in substance use and behavioral problems. Darling used cross-sectional and longitudinal data collected from 3,761 students across six California high schools, analyzing extracurricular participation alongside grades, academic aspirations, stress, substance use, and other demographic characteristics. This source is important because it provides empirical evidence showing that extracurricular participation is linked to positive academic and behavioral outcomes across different demographic groups. This resource directly supports our thesis by explaining how demographic and socioeconomic differences influence access to extracurricular participation and how participation itself is connected to educational engagement and student adjustment. It is especially useful for examining which factors predict extracurricular involvement and how participation may shape students' academic experiences.

Feraco, Tommaso, et al. “An Integrated Model of School Students’ Academic Achievement and Life Satisfaction. Linking Soft Skills, Extracurricular Activities, Self-Regulated Learning, Motivation, and Emotions.” European Journal of Psychology of Education, vol. 38, no. 1, March 2023, pp. 109-30, https://doi.org/10.1007/s10212-022-00601-4.

Feraco et al. (2023) built an integrated model that links soft skills, extracurricular activities, self-regulated learning, motivation, achievement emotions, cognitive ability, academic achievement, and life satisfaction. They collect data from Italian students in grades 5-12, measuring years of involvement in activities, soft skills, academic motivation, emotions, and self-regulated learning, and test scores. Using Bayesian path analysis, Feraco et al. (2023) find that extracurricular participation is positively related to soft skills and cognitive abilities, and that soft skills are directly associated with positive emotions, self-regulated learning, motivation, and life satisfaction. This article is important because it shows that extracurricular activities help build soft skills and cognitive resources that promote both school performance and overall life satisfaction. For our project, it strengthens the claim that unequal access to extracurriculars by socioeconomic status, gender, or race can translate into unequal academic achievement and well-being, since activities are one pathway through which students develop the soft skills and study behaviors that support long-term success.

Goshin, Mikhail, et al. “The Strategies of Parental Involvement in Adolescents’ Education and Extracurricular Activities.” Journal of Youth and Adolescence, vol. 50, no. 5, May 2021, pp. 906-20, https://doi.org/10.1007/s10964-021-01399-y.

Goshin et al. (2021) looks at how different strategies of parental involvement in education effect students' participation in extracurricular activities, addressing a gap on how home based parenting practices connect to out-of-school activity engagement. According to survey data from 3887 Russian parents of children attending general education institutions, the authors identify three parental strategies: “Intrusive” (deep involvement and maximum control), “Supervisory” (facilitative support with freedom for the adolescent), and “Detached” (superficial/absent involvement). They argue that these strategies produce different patterns of extracurricular participation depending on the child's age and the type of activity. They found “Intrusive” parenting linked to the highest participation in primary school but reported a sharp drop off as children enter secondary and high school, while “Supervisory” parenting is most strongly associated with continued participation, autonomous choice of activities, and engagement in academic and social activities like volunteering. Children of “Detached” parents participate least overall and are more likely to choose sports over academic enrichment, and receive significantly less financial support for activities. The authors also find that parental education level are strongly tied to parenting strategy with more educated and culturally resourced parents disproportionately represented in the “Supervisory” group. This source is important for our project because it adds a parenting factor to the inequality story, suggesting that disparities in extracurricular participation are shaped not only by structural factors like SES and family structure (as in An & Western, 2019, and Castillo, 2024) but also by the quality and style of parental engagement. A key limitation is that the study is set entirely in Russia, where the extracurricular and cultural expectations around parenting differ from America, so the generalizability might need further testing/research.

Gossin, Françoise Pasche, and Éléonore Simon. “Un Aménagement de l’horaire Pour Une Meilleure Gestion Des Activités d’éducation Scolaire et Extra-Scolaire : Une Question de Partenariat, d’attentes et d’équité.” Éducation et Socialisation, vol. 63, March 2022, https://doi.org/10.4000/edso.17924.

Gossin and Simon (2022) explore how school schedules and partnerships between schools, families, and outside organizations can help students better balance school and extracurricular activities. The study focuses on questions of fairness, expectations, and equal access to extracurricular opportunities. The authors discuss how differences in family resources, school systems, and scheduling can affect which students are able to participate in activities outside the classroom. Gossin and Simon (2022) argue that stronger coordination between schools and community organizations can help create more equal opportunities for students to engage in both educational and extracurricular experiences. This article is important because it highlights that access to extracurricular activities is not equal for all students and is often shaped by larger social and structural factors. For our project, this source supports the idea that inequalities in extracurricular participation are connected to issues of equity, family support, and school policies, which can influence students’ academic and personal development.

Heath, Ryan D, et al. “Extracurricular Activities and Disadvantaged Youth: A Complicated—But Promising—Story.” Urban Education, vol. 57, no. 8, October 2022, pp. 1415-49, https://doi.org/10.1177/0042085918805797.

The literature review by Heath et al. reviews studies published between 1980 and 2017 to assess youth extracurricular participation through one of four dimensions: gender, socioeconomic status, race, and immigrant status. Across these studies, they find that disadvantaged youth are less likely to participate in extracurricular activities, but experience greater social and academic benefits from engaging in them. Specifically, the review presents consistent findings across the literature, including that youth typically prefer extracurriculars that align with gender norms and that participation may be especially beneficial for low-income children and youth of color. This highlights that access is a primary issue in extracurricular engagement and that supporting the expansion of programs can address this issue. This source offers an important foundation for our research, as it provides a broad overview of the disparities in extracurricular participation while also emphasizing the potential benefits for marginalized youth. Additionally, the authors also identify gaps in the literature around intersectionality, which is a useful insight for our own analysis.

Li, Jiarong. The Research about the Fifth & Sixth Grade Students’ Extracurricular Activites Participations Motive, Interpersonal Relationship and Well-Being in Yilan City. 2009, https://search.library.ucla.edu/permalink/01UCS_LAL/1up42fr/cdi_ndltd_primary_oai_union_ndltd_org_TW_097FGU05205011.

Li (2009) looks at how extracurricular activities affect students’ relationships, motivations, and overall happiness among fifth and sixth grade students in Yilan City. The study focuses on why students choose to participate in activities and how those experiences shape their social lives and emotional well-being. Using survey data from students, Li (2009) finds that students who are more involved in extracurricular activities often have stronger friendships, better social connections, and higher levels of well-being. The research also shows that students who join activities because of personal interest, enjoyment, or social connection tend to gain more positive experiences from participating. This article is important because it shows that extracurricular activities can help students feel more connected, confident, and emotionally supported at a young age. For our project, this source supports the idea that having access to extracurricular opportunities can positively impact students’ social development, happiness, and overall well-being over time.

Morris, David S. “Extracurricular Activity Participation in High School: Mechanisms Linking Participation to Math Achievement and 4-Year College Attendance.” American Educational Research Journal, vol. 53, no. 5, October 2016, pp. 1376-410, https://doi.org/10.3102/0002831216667579.

Morris argues that extracurricular activity participation in high school increases math achievement and four year college attendance by strengthening students’ social connections, academic engagement and involvement in school. The study uses longitudinal data from the Education Longitudinal Study of 2002 (ELS), analyzing student responses from the US to examine how extracurricular participation, family income, noncognitive skills and demographic characteristics influence math achievement. This resource is important because it explains how extracurricular participation contributes to long term academic success and why there are benefits. This research supports our thesis as it addresses the role of family income in extracurricular participation as well as suggesting that students with greater school involvement/engagement are more likely to benefit from extracurriculars.

Meier, Ann, et al. “A Quarter Century of Participation in School-Based Extracurricular Activities: Inequalities by Race, Class, Gender and Age?” Journal of Youth and Adolescence, vol. 47, no. 6, June 2018, pp. 1299-316, https://doi.org/10.1007/s10964-018-0838-1.

Meier et al. (2018) examines 27 years of trends in school based extracurricular participation in the U.S., analyzing how participation patterns differ by race/ethnicity, social class, gender, and grade level, and how participation relates to academic outcomes and substance use. Looking at Monitoring the Future data of 500k+ people from 1986 to 2013, the authors use multinomial logistic regression to classify youth into four mutually exclusive participation profiles (no participation, sport only, non-sport only, and both sport and non-sport) and test interactions between demographic characteristics and survey year to assess how gaps have evolved over time. They argue that while extracurricular activities are able to support positive development, school based participation has become a source of growing inequality with the social class gap widening over time even in a setting that should be very accessible. Their major findings show that kids with a college educated parent are more than twice as likely to participate in both sport and non-sport activities compared to those without, and this advantage has gotten worse across the 27 year period. Black students have fallen further behind other racial/ethnic groups in non-sport participation and girls have passed boys in overall and cross-domain participation. Participation is also very consistently linked to higher grades, stronger college graduation expectations, and lower chance of cigarette, alcohol, and marijuana use. This source is important for our project because it provides the longest historical analysis of school based extracurricular participation using nationally representative U.S. data. It also directly helps answer our core question about how race, class, gender, and age shape access to extracurriculars and the academic outcomes that follow. The authors’ distinction between school based and out-of-school activities is also important for our research. A limitation is that the MTF data doesn’t look at out-of-school or pay-to-play activities which the authors state are likely where the biggest class divides happen.

O’Flaherty, Martin, et al. “Do Extracurricular Activities Contribute to Better Adolescent Outcomes? A Fixed‐effects Panel Data Approach.” Journal of Adolescence (London, England.) [Hoboken], vol. 94, no. 6, August 2022, pp. 855-66, https://doi.org/10.1002/jad.12069.

O’Flaherty argues that extracurricular activity participation contributes to positive academic and social outcomes such as better academic outcomes, fewer behavioral problems, and reduced risk of substance use in children and young adults. This study uses a fixed-effects panel data approach, which tracks the participants over time (only comparing within individuals) to control for any confounding variables that may influence outcomes. This is an Australian longitudinal study that used data collected from 3,885 adolescents around 12-15 years old. This article is important because it shows us that extracurricular activities do show positive effects instead of just stating that students who have an advantage are already more likely to participate in extracurriculars. This research supports our thesis as it shows that family resources and inequality play a role in access to extracurriculars and it supports our question on how extracurriculars tie into academic achievement over time.

Sena, Lawrence C. A Quantitative Correlation Study on the Relationship between Navajo High School Students' Participation in Extracurricular Activities and Academic Success, National University, United States -- California, 2024. ProQuest, https://www.proquest.com/dissertations-theses/quantitative-correlation-study-on-relationship/docview/3245313787/se-2.

Sena argues that participation in extracurricular activities is positively associated with academic success among Navajo high school students, namely through higher GPAs and improved assessment scores. The study used quantitative correlational research and regression analysis on data collected from ninety-one senior high school students at Southeastern Navajo Nation high school, examining extracurricular participation, GPA, and demographic factors. The source is important because it focuses specifically on Native American students, a population that is often underrepresented in research about extracurricular participation and education achievement. This resource supports our thesis by showing how socioeconomic conditions, demographic factors, in this case focusing on Navajo students who are historically underrepresented in the US, can influence students' access to extracurricular activities and therefore their educational outcomes.

Shaffer, Michael L. Education Beyond the Academic World: The Importance and Impact of Co-Curricular and Extracurricular Activities for High School Students, Robert Morris University, United States -- Pennsylvania, 2019. ProQuest, https://www.proquest.com/dissertations-theses/education-beyond-academic-world-importance-impact/docview/2388704502/se-2.

Schaffer argues that co-curricular and extracurricular activities positively influence high school students’ academic performance, attendance, motivation and future success, and therefore remain an important part of education. This study used a mixed-methods, including quantitative data on GPA, attendance, graduation rates, and test scores along with surveys and interviews from students and faculty. This resource is important because it provides extensive research on how extracurricular and co-curricular participation affects both academic and personal development in modern high school education. It also discusses the consequences of reducing these programs. This resource supports our thesis by showing that extracurricular activities contribute to higher academic performance and stronger school engagement. It is especially useful because it connects student success to broader education and social factors including, motivation, school support, and opportunity access.

Wang, Weihao, et al. “The Relationship between Participation in Extracurricular Arts and Sports Activities and Adolescents’ Social and Emotional Skills: An Empirical Analysis Based on the OECD Social and Emotional Skills Survey.” Behavioral Sciences, vol. 14, no. 7, June 2024, p. 541, https://doi.org/10.3390/bs14070541.

Wang et al. find that participation in sports- and arts-based extracurriculars have a positive impact on social emotional skills including task performance, emotional regulation, collaboration, open mindedness, and engagement with others. This study utilizes an OECD survey of 7268 10-year-olds and 15-year-olds in Suzhou, China and groups students based on their participation in arts, sports, both or neither. The authors find that arts-only participation is associated with higher levels of engagement with others and open-mindedness, while sports participation is linked to higher collaboration, engagement with others, and open-mindedness. Students engaged in both types of activities perform significantly higher across all measured social-emotional skill domains compared to those who do not participate in extracurriculars. This source provides evidence that the type of extracurricular activity has an impact on the benefits that youth experience, making it important to research patterns in type of extracurricular engagement. However, because the study is conducted in China, further research is needed to determine if it is generalizable to the United States due to different education systems and cultural expectations.

Verner‐Filion, Jérémie, et al. “Extracurricular Activities—Extra Beneficial: The Role of Motivation for Extracurricular Activities on Outcomes in High‐School Students.” Journal of Adolescence, vol. 97, no. 7, October 2025, pp. 1869-81, https://doi.org/10.1002/jad.70008.

Verner-Filion et al. (2025) use the Self Determination Theory to study how motivation for extracurricular activities relates to high school students’ academic and psychological outcomes. The authors analyze survey data from students in the United States, Canada, China, and France who reported on their motivation for extracurriculars, school motivation, psychological need, and dropout intentions. Teachers also rated a subsample of students on motivation, self-efficacy, optimism, and the number of missed school days. The study finds that self-determined motivation for extracurricular activities is linked to better academic functioning and well-being. These effects appear across countries and are visible in both student and teacher reports, which makes the evidence strong for different school systems. This article is important because it shifts attention from simply doing activities to how strongly and autonomously students want to participate, showing that high-quality motivation connects extracurricular life with school engagement. For our project, it helps answer how extracurricular participation is tied to academic involvement and achievement by showing that motivated participation supports school motivation, psychological needs, and attendance-related behaviors.

Xiao, Bowen, et al. “Shyness, Extracurricular Activity Engagement, and Internalizing Problems Among Emerging Adults in University.” Canadian Journal of Behavioural Science, vol. 56, no. 4, October 2024, pp. 435-44, https://doi.org/10.1037/cbs0000389.

Xiao et al. (2024) explore how shyness, extracurricular activities, and mental health are connected among university students. The researchers focus on emerging adults and look at whether being involved in extracurricular activities can help reduce feelings of anxiety, stress, and depression that shy students may experience. Using survey data from university students, Xiao et al. (2024) find that students who are more shy are more likely to struggle with internalizing problems, but students who participate more in extracurricular activities tend to have better emotional well-being and fewer mental health challenges. The study suggests that extracurricular activities can help shy students build confidence, create social connections, and feel more supported. This article is important because it shows that extracurricular activities are not just about academics or building resumes, but can also play a major role in students’ mental health and personal growth. For our project, this source supports the idea that unequal access to extracurricular opportunities can affect not only academic success, but also emotional well-being and social development.
