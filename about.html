About This Project

How We Built Our Digital Humanities Project

Project Overview

Our project examines children’s extracurricular participation in the United States using the U.S. Census Bureau’s Survey of Income and Program Participation (SIPP). We are interested in how access to activities such as sports, clubs, lessons, and religious groups reflects broader inequalities in educational opportunity.

Although extracurricular activities are often treated as optional or separate from school, our project considers how family income, parental education, family structure, race/ethnicity, gender, and region may shape whether children are able to participate.

Our larger research question is: Are extracurricular activities truly “extra,” or do they reveal deeper inequalities in children’s access to educational and developmental opportunities?

Sources

Our main source is the 2024 Survey of Income and Program Participation (SIPP), a nationally representative survey administered by the U.S. Census Bureau. The dataset includes information about income, employment, household structure, demographics, government program participation, and child well-being. For our project, we are especially interested in variables related to children ages 6 to 17 and their participation in extracurricular activities.

We are also using scholarly sources from our annotated bibliography to understand how extracurricular participation relates to academic achievement, social development, emotional well-being, family resources, and educational inequality.

Processing

For the processing stage, our group will select the SIPP variables most relevant to our research questions. These may include extracurricular participation, household income, poverty status, parental education, family structure, race/ethnicity, sex, age, and Census region.

Because the SIPP dataset is very large, we will subset it to focus on children ages 6 to 17 and the variables most directly connected to our topic. We will also consider the limitations of parent-reported data and broad activity categories when interpreting our findings.

Presentation

Our website will present our findings through a written narrative, data visualizations, a map, a timeline, and supporting images. Rather than presenting charts as isolated results, we will connect each visualization to a specific humanities question about childhood, education, inequality, and access.

Our goal is to make the data understandable to a general audience while also showing that datasets are not neutral. The way SIPP organizes children, households, and activities shapes what can be seen and what remains hidden.
